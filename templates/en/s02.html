Young adults and housing

Sharing a flat

stopped being a

phase.

The share of people in their late twenties and early thirties living with housemates has roughly tripled in twenty years, and the age of moving into a place of your own has shifted from the mid-twenties to the mid-thirties.
It happened one lease at a time, with nothing to announce — which is why it read for years as a run of individual choices rather than as a pattern.

“Roughly tripled” slides straight past. It is worth stopping on the actual shape of it, because here the size is the story.

Numbers

1 in 3

people aged 25 to 34 now live with housemates. Twenty years ago it was 1 in 8.

The average age of moving into a place of your own has gone from 27 to 34, and rent has climbed from roughly 28 to 41 per cent of income.
It is not spread evenly: steepest in large cities, close to flat in smaller towns, so the national figure averages two very different situations.

Every generation gets told the one before it had it harder. This is one of the rare arguments where the comparison can actually be checked.

Then vs Now

A 30-year-old in 2005 and a 30-year-old now want roughly the same things, and surveys asking that question get the same answers in both decades. What changed is the arithmetic.
What looks from the outside like a change in preferences is a plan being postponed rather than abandoned — and from the outside those two look identical.

Postponing something by a couple of years is an inconvenience. Postponing it by a decade starts dragging other things back with it.

Why This Matters

Living with housemates at 32 is not in itself a hardship. What follows it is. Leaving shared housing has moved from 27 to 34, and a decade of renting is a decade accumulating nothing.
The two curves move together closely enough, across enough countries, that most demographers treat them as connected rather than coincidental.

Which leaves the question the numbers cannot answer by themselves. Something caused this, and the people who study it do not fully agree on what.

The Arguments

Supply

We stopped building enough homes for the people who need them.
If it is supply, the fix is to build.

Credit & Work

Deposits outran wages, so saving never catches up.
If it is credit you tax; if it is work, neither touches it.
These are less three rival explanations than three parts of one. What almost nobody argues is that it reverses on its own — it has run the same direction for twenty years through several economic cycles.

That is the trend as it stands — measured clearly, explained three ways, and not turning around by itself.
